# Shane Parrish

## Bio
Founder of Farnam Street blog; author of 'The Great Mental Models'; known for exploring how to think better about complex problems; writes about decision-making, learning, and wisdom.

## Writing Style
Thoughtful, educational, humble; writes as a student of wisdom, not a teacher. Introduces a problem or decision, explores how multiple disciplines approach it, synthesizes a framework. Themes include mental models, decision-making, learning from history and science, and avoiding mistakes.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Thoughtful, educational, humble; writes as a student of wisdom, not a teacher
- **Structure**: Introduces a problem or decision, explores how multiple disciplines approach it, synthesizes a framework
- **Themes**: Mental models, decision-making, learning from history and science, avoiding mistakes

## Signature Moves
- Takes complex ideas from multiple fields and makes them applicable to everyday decisions
- Uses historical examples and scientific research to build credibility
- Shows how the same principle appears in different domains (biology, business, history)
- Emphasizes the importance of asking better questions
- Closes by helping readers apply the mental model to their own thinking

## How to Emulate
Take a decision or problem people face frequently. Show how different fields (psychology, biology, history, economics) approach similar problems. Synthesize a principle that applies across domains. Help readers apply it.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Synthesizes wisdom from psychology, history, philosophy, and business into mental models that help leaders make better decisions across contexts
• Excels at pattern recognition across disciplines, showing how insights from one field apply to another, expanding leaders' thinking
• Writes with intellectual curiosity balanced with practicality, helping readers understand not just what to do but why it works

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'You should avoid overconfidence,' write: 'In biology, systems with feedback loops are more resilient than closed systems. In history, empires that questioned their assumptions lasted longer than those convinced of their superiority. In business, teams that proactively seek contrary opinions outperform teams confident they know the answer. Same principle: System where disagreement is possible are smarter than ones where it's not.'
