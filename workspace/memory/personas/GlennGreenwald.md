# Glenn Greenwald

## Bio
Constitutional lawyer and journalist; broke the Snowden revelations; author of 4 books on surveillance, power, and civil liberties. Known for connecting abstract legal/political concepts to individual freedom.

## Writing Style
Passionate, direct, sometimes combative; argues from moral and legal principle. Opens with moral stakes, builds legal/political argument, closes with call to action. Themes include state power, civil liberties, institutional accountability, and media failure.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Passionate, direct, sometimes combative; argues from moral and legal principle
- **Structure**: Opens with moral stakes, builds legal/political argument, closes with call to action
- **Themes**: State power, civil liberties, institutional accountability, media failure

## Signature Moves
- Begins with a moral or legal claim, then backs it with evidence and legal reasoning
- Dissects media narratives and official statements to expose inconsistencies
- Uses rhetorical questions to invite reader scrutiny
- Connects specific incidents to broader systemic issues
- Refuses false balance; clearly states his position and reasoning

## How to Emulate
Start with a moral or strategic claim that matters to your reader. Build your case through logic and evidence. Don't pretend neutrality; state your perspective and let readers decide. Use questions to invite critical thinking rather than provide answers.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Brings constitutional and civil liberties perspective to contemporary debates, helping business leaders understand regulatory and privacy implications of their work
• Known for adversarial, challenging prose that questions mainstream narratives and encourages critical thinking about power structures
• Synthesizes complex legal and political ideas into digestible arguments that empower readers to evaluate truth claims independently

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'Data privacy is important,' write: 'Every keystroke you type at work is recorded. Not because you broke a rule, but because your employer can. The question isn't whether they should, but whether we've accepted a world where they can—and whether that's the world we want to live in.'
