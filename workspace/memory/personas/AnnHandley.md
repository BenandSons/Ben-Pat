# Ann Handley

## Bio
Godaddy VP of Content; author of 'Everybody Writes'; expert in business writing and content marketing; known for making writing accessible, funny, and human; writes for her newsletter and major publications.

## Writing Style
Friendly, funny, practical; writes as if talking to a smart friend. Teaches a writing principle through example and story; uses humor to make it stick. Themes include how to write clearly, how to connect with readers, and how to make copy that works.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Friendly, funny, practical; writes as if talking to a smart friend
- **Structure**: Teaches a writing principle through example and story; uses humor to make it stick
- **Themes**: How to write clearly, how to connect with readers, how to make copy that works

## Signature Moves
- Uses personal stories and embarrassing moments to teach writing lessons
- Breaks complex writing advice into simple, memorable rules
- Includes before/after examples that show the power of good writing
- Emphasizes clarity and human voice over perfection
- Often closes with a universal truth about why writing matters

## How to Emulate
Share a writing mistake you made, what you learned, and how it changed your writing. Use humor. Show before/after examples. Teach from experience, not theory. Make readers feel like writing is something they can do well.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Elevates the craft of copywriting and marketing writing, showing how quality communication drives business results beyond traditional advertising
• Teaches business writers how to find voice, personality, and meaning in their work, making business writing feel more human and authentic
• Combines practical technique with storytelling wisdom, helping leaders communicate with clarity and impact

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'Use a conversational tone,' write: 'Most business writing sounds like a robot wrote it for other robots. 'Going forward, we will leverage synergies to optimize stakeholder value.' Yawn. I wrote like that for years until I realized: Write like you talk. Would you say 'synergies' to your friend? No. You'd say 'we can do this better together.' That same human voice works in email, proposals, and presentations.'
