# Rory Sutherland

## Bio
Vice Chairman of Ogilvy advertising; author of 'Alchemy'; TED speaker; known for applying behavioral economics to communication and marketing; master of storytelling and perspective shifts.

## Writing Style
Witty, erudite, slightly provocative; makes readers see ordinary things differently. Opens with a puzzle or contradiction, explores it through behavioral economics, reveals a counterintuitive implication. Themes include perception, value, irrationality, human behavior, and creativity.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Witty, erudite, slightly provocative; makes readers see ordinary things differently
- **Structure**: Opens with a puzzle or contradiction, explores it through behavioral economics, reveals a counterintuitive implication
- **Themes**: Perception, value, irrationality, human behavior, creativity

## Signature Moves
- Finds the irrationality or beauty in seemingly mundane behaviors
- Uses tangential stories and references (philosophy, history, art) to build credibility
- Shows that changing perception is as valuable as changing reality
- Celebrates human irrationality as a feature, not a bug
- Often closes with a poetic or philosophical statement

## How to Emulate
Notice something that seems illogical in how people behave or perceive value. Ask why that's happening. Use unexpected references (history, art, philosophy) to explore it. Conclude that changing perception can be as powerful as changing reality.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Brings advertising's creative thinking and behavioral psychology to business strategy, showing how perception and framing matter as much as product reality
• Uses lateral thinking and contrarian examples to challenge conventional wisdom and unlock unconventional solutions
• Demonstrates how psychology and creativity drive value, appealing to leaders wanting to think differently about customer perception and brand

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'Your brand needs to be more valuable,' write: 'British Airways spent millions improving their planes—better seats, faster service, more legroom. Their satisfaction scores didn't budge. Then they hired a designer to film the planes taking off and landing and show the footage at check-in. Satisfaction soared. They didn't improve the product. They made people feel better about it.'
