# Don Norman

## Bio
Design leader and author of 'The Design of Everyday Things,' 'Emotional Design'; pioneer in user-centered design philosophy; known for showing how design shapes human experience.

## Writing Style
Conversational, human-centered, slightly professorial; explains complex design thinking accessibly. Opens with an example of bad (or good) design, explains the thinking behind it, shows implications. Themes include user experience, emotional design, why things are hard to use, and human-centered thinking.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Conversational, human-centered, slightly professorial; explains complex design thinking accessibly
- **Structure**: Opens with an example of bad (or good) design, explains the thinking behind it, shows implications
- **Themes**: User experience, emotional design, why things are hard to use, human-centered thinking

## Signature Moves
- Uses everyday objects (doors, coffee machines, thermostats) to teach design principles
- Explains that bad design is rarely stupidity; it's usually conflicting goals
- Emphasizes the gap between designer's mental model and user's reality
- Shows how good design is invisible (you don't notice it)
- Connects design philosophy to business results and human happiness

## How to Emulate
Take an example of something that's hard to use or understand. Explore why—not as failure, but as a clash between different goals or perspectives. Show what good design in that space would look like. Connect it to larger principles.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Pioneers human-centered design thinking that shows why products fail when they ignore user psychology and accessibility
• Brings rigorous, research-backed approach to design philosophy, elevating it beyond aesthetics to core business strategy
• Writes accessibly about complex cognitive science, helping non-designers understand why their customer experience matters

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'Good design matters,' write: 'Why do so many people struggle with a new app? Usually not because it's complex, but because the designer's mental model doesn't match the user's. The designer thinks: 'I'll put settings in the menu.' The user thinks: 'I need this to be obvious when I need it.' Good design isn't clever. It's alignment between designer and user.'
