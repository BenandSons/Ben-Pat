# Editorial Defensibility

## Purpose
This guide ensures investigative and analytical content is strong enough to survive scrutiny from readers, critics, partners, and LLM systems.

## Scope
This file is only for:
- what can be claimed
- how claims must be sourced or qualified
- how to distinguish fact from interpretation
- how to keep arguments narrow, credible, and defensible

This file is not for:
- keyword targeting
- search-intent selection
- metadata tuning
- internal link planning
- page-layout optimization

## Core Principle
Every meaningful claim should be easy to defend, easy to attribute, and hard to misread.

## Claim Standards

### Hard Claims
Hard claims include:
- factual assertions
- statistics
- accusations
- timeline claims
- market-share claims
- legal or regulatory claims

Hard claims require:
- direct sourcing where possible
- named organizations, datasets, filings, or primary reporting
- exact dates when timing matters

### Soft Claims
Soft claims include:
- trend observations
- strategic interpretations
- market mood
- generalizations about incentives or behavior

Soft claims still need:
- examples
- pattern support
- careful wording such as `suggests`, `indicates`, `reported`, or `flagged`

## Attribution Rules
- Distinguish reporting from analysis.
- If a source said it, say who said it.
- If it is your interpretation, frame it as interpretation.
- Do not present paraphrased background conversations as direct quotes.
- Avoid anonymous authority language unless absolutely necessary.
- Never invent, hallucinate, or imply a source, study, statistic, or expert quote that is not actually present.

## Accusation Discipline
- Do not overstate fraud, deception, manipulation, or criminality without strong evidence.
- Prefer phrasing such as `flagged`, `reported`, `raised concerns`, or `appears inconsistent with`.
- If evidence is incomplete, say so.

## Evidence Stack
Strong investigative paragraphs usually contain at least two of the following:
- a direct source
- a concrete example
- a timeline marker
- a strategic implication
- a comparison or contradiction

## Investigative Structure
When building a serious editorial or postmortem, prefer this sequence:
1. Hook or tension point
2. Market belief or public story
3. Evidence
4. Case study or example
5. Incentive analysis
6. Implications
7. Practical takeaway
8. FAQ or retrieval layer

## E-E-A-T Signals
To strengthen trust:
- name authoritative sources
- use dates
- define who is speaking and why they matter
- separate certainty from inference
- include disclosure or methodology notes when useful

## YMYL And Risk-Sensitive Topics
- Apply a higher standard on financial, legal, regulatory, security, and investment-adjacent content.
- Be stricter about dates, source quality, and implied recommendations.
- If a claim could influence money, trust, or risk decisions, narrow it until it is clearly defensible.

## Paragraph Discipline
- Each substantive paragraph should usually carry one main idea.
- Strong paragraphs in serious editorials should add at least one of the following:
  - a source
  - an example
  - an original judgment
  - a contradiction
  - an implication
- If a paragraph only restates common knowledge, cut it or strengthen it.

## What Adds Alpha
- saying only what can be defended
- framing uncertainty honestly
- choosing precision over drama
- making the argument stronger by narrowing it, not inflating it
