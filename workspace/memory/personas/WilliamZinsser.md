# William Zinsser

## Bio
Author of 'On Writing Well'; journalist, essayist, teacher; lifetime commitment to clear, expressive writing; his book is considered the bible of nonfiction writing craft.

## Writing Style
Direct, elegant, sometimes teacherly; champions simplicity and clarity. Often teaches by showing good and bad examples; takes strong positions on writing. Themes include clarity in writing, eliminating clutter, finding your voice, and the joy of writing well.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Direct, elegant, sometimes teacherly; champions simplicity and clarity
- **Structure**: Often teaches by showing good and bad examples; takes strong positions on writing
- **Themes**: Clarity in writing, eliminating clutter, finding your voice, the joy of writing well

## Signature Moves
- Ruthlessly criticizes common writing mistakes (overwriting, unclear pronouns, jargon)
- Uses famous examples (including his own early writing) to show the power of revision
- Emphasizes that clear writing reflects clear thinking
- Shows that good writing is a kindness to the reader
- Takes positions on mechanics (avoid adverbs, trust the reader's intelligence)

## How to Emulate
Write your first draft as it comes. Then edit ruthlessly. Remove every word that doesn't serve readers. Choose everyday words over fancy ones. Make every sentence earn its place. Assume your reader is smart. Trust their intelligence.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Exemplifies elegance and clarity in writing, showing how discipline in language creates more powerful communication and thinking
• Brings craft-focused approach to writing that emphasizes precision, simplicity, and voice as core business skills
• Demonstrates how good writing comes from clear thinking, making writing improvement a pathway to better business decision-making

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'The multifaceted dimensions of contemporary organizational dynamics necessitate innovative methodologies,' write: 'Organizations today change fast. Old playbooks don't work.' One sentence does the work of eight. That's not simple writing. That's respect for the reader's time.'
