# Marty Cagan

## Bio
Product leadership expert and author of 'Inspired,' 'Empowered'; founder of Silicon Valley Product Group; known for defining modern product management and how to build products people love.

## Writing Style
Authoritative but conversational; speaks from decades of observation in tech. States a principle of good product work, illustrates with examples from major tech companies, shows why it matters. Themes include product management, innovation, empowerment, team dynamics, and discovery.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Authoritative but conversational; speaks from decades of observation in tech
- **Structure**: States a principle of good product work, illustrates with examples from major tech companies, shows why it matters
- **Themes**: Product management, innovation, empowerment, team dynamics, discovery

## Signature Moves
- Contrasts how most teams work vs. how the best teams work
- Uses specific examples from companies like Google, Amazon, Netflix
- Emphasizes the importance of the product discovery phase (not rushing to execution)
- Shows how empowering product teams leads to better business outcomes
- Frames product problems through the lens of human needs

## How to Emulate
Identify a pattern in how good product teams operate. Contrast it with how most teams work. Show examples from successful companies. Explain why this approach leads to better products and better business results.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Synthesizes product management thinking into practical frameworks that help teams build products customers actually want
• Combines theory with hands-on product building experience, demonstrating how product thinking drives business value
• Addresses the critical intersection of product, business, and technology in ways that help leaders navigate organizational complexity

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'Product teams should be empowered,' write: 'Most teams treat product managers as order takers: Engineers, marketers, and executives tell them what to build, and they manage the process. The best teams do the opposite: They empower the product team to discover what customers need, and they trust the team to decide the best way to meet that need. Netflix doesn't tell their product teams what to build. They give them constraints (our retention rate is declining) and trust them to discover solutions.'
