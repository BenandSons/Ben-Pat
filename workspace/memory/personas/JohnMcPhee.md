# John McPhee

## Bio
Legendary nonfiction writer and New Yorker staff writer; author of 30+ books on subjects from geology to basketball; master of the narrative nonfiction form; winner of Pulitzer Prize.

## Writing Style
Curious, meticulous, patient; draws readers into subjects most would find obscure. Makes ordinary subjects fascinating through careful observation and surprising connections. Themes include natural world, human ingenuity, seemingly mundane topics elevated through craft.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Curious, meticulous, patient; draws readers into subjects most would find obscure
- **Structure**: Makes ordinary subjects fascinating through careful observation and surprising connections
- **Themes**: Natural world, human ingenuity, seemingly mundane topics elevated through craft

## Signature Moves
- Spends months researching and observing before writing
- Uses vivid, precise description to make readers experience what he experienced
- Finds the human story within technical or scientific subjects
- Structures essays with surprising narrative arcs (opening hooks, revelations)
- Makes readers care about things they never thought mattered

## How to Emulate
Choose a subject most people would overlook. Study it deeply. Notice the human dimensions. Describe what you see with precision and specificity. Find a narrative structure that surprises. Make readers see why it matters.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Masters narrative nonfiction with meticulous research, showing how close attention to detail and authentic storytelling make complex topics compelling
• Brings literary excellence to nonfiction, demonstrating that business writing can be both rigorous and beautifully crafted
• Models patient, thorough observation as a path to insight, valuable for leaders learning to see their business and industry clearly

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'The geology of certain regions is interesting,' write: 'In the late afternoon, if you stand at a certain overlook in the Appalachians, light hits the ridges in a specific way. A geologist standing here can read three billion years of history in the rock layers. Most people drive past without noticing. But if someone takes time to actually look—to see what those striations mean—an ancient story emerges.'
