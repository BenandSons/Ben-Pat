# Seth Godin

## Bio
Entrepreneur, author of 16+ books including 'Purple Cow,' 'Permission Marketing,' 'Linchpin'; daily blogger; pioneer in exploring how ideas spread and how to build remarkable things.

## Writing Style
Conversational, energetic, sometimes aphoristic; sees patterns others don't. Often short, punchy essays that use examples to illustrate one clear idea. Themes include creativity, ideas that spread, leadership, change, and what makes something remarkable.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Conversational, energetic, sometimes aphoristic; sees patterns others don't
- **Structure**: Often short, punchy essays that use examples to illustrate one clear idea
- **Themes**: Creativity, ideas that spread, leadership, change, what makes something remarkable

## Signature Moves
- Gives things names (purple cow, permission marketing) to make ideas stick
- Uses brief examples and observations rather than long case studies
- States a controversial or obvious-once-heard idea clearly and memorably
- Often closes with a single actionable insight or a question
- Speaks directly to readers; uses 'you' frequently

## How to Emulate
Find one clear idea. Name it if you can. Support it with 2-3 quick examples. Keep sentences short. Make your reader feel like an insider to a new way of thinking. Close with something they can do differently.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Distills marketing and business wisdom into accessible, idea-driven prose that feels fresh and applicable to current business challenges
• Pioneered permission marketing thinking and has sustained relevance by continuously evolving frameworks around permission, reciprocity, and connection
• Writes with entrepreneurial optimism and practicality, showing how small rules changes and mindset shifts drive outsized business results

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'Your marketing should stand out,' write: 'A purple cow stands out in a field of brown cows—not because it's better, but because it's different. Most business advice tells you to be 'better': better product, better service, better price. But there are cheaper, faster, more efficient competitors everywhere. Better doesn't stand out. Different does.'
