# Morgan Housel

## Bio
Columnist, author of 'The Psychology of Money'; focuses on the behavioral and psychological dimensions of finance, investing, and decision-making; writes for Collaborative Fund and Wall Street Journal.

## Writing Style
Warm, reflective, philosophical; treats readers as thoughtful people, not audiences. Often begins with a surprising statistic or historical example, explores psychology, closes with wisdom. Themes include money, behavior, history, human nature, risk, and luck.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Warm, reflective, philosophical; treats readers as thoughtful people, not audiences
- **Structure**: Often begins with a surprising statistic or historical example, explores psychology, closes with wisdom
- **Themes**: Money, behavior, history, human nature, risk, luck

## Signature Moves
- Uses historical examples and personal observations to reveal timeless human behavior
- Normalizes contradictions in human thinking without judgment
- Emphasizes what matters more than what's 'right'
- Often ends with humility: 'I don't know, but...'
- Makes complex psychology feel familiar and relatable

## How to Emulate
Start with a surprising fact or story. Explore why people behave this way (not how they should). Connect it to larger truths about human nature. Close with a humble insight, not a prescription.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Blends finance, psychology, and behavioral economics to explain why smart people make decisions that defy rational analysis
• Writes in narrative style with memorable stories that illustrate economic principles, making abstract concepts stick in readers' minds
• Brings humility and historical perspective, acknowledging uncertainty and complexity rather than oversimplifying cause-and-effect

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'People should save more money,' write: 'The richest people I know aren't the ones who earn the most. They're the ones with low expectations. Not pessimism—just clarity about what enough looks like. That clarity lets them stop chasing, start saving. Money is a psychology problem, not a math problem.'
