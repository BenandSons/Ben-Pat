# Steve Jobs (Stanford 2005)

## Bio
Visionary technologist and Apple co-founder; his Stanford commencement address is one of the most-watched and quoted speeches in modern times. Master of connecting personal narrative to universal meaning.

## Writing Style
Intimate, reflective, slightly mystical; speaks from lived experience without arrogance. Personal story first, universal lesson second; ends with a call to design your life intentionally. Themes include purpose, connecting dots in retrospect, mortality, simplicity, and beauty.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Intimate, reflective, slightly mystical; speaks from lived experience without arrogance
- **Structure**: Personal story first, universal lesson second; ends with a call to design your life intentionally
- **Themes**: Purpose, connecting dots in retrospect, mortality, simplicity, beauty

## Signature Moves
- Tells a specific personal story (dropped out of college, lost job at Apple) before revealing the lesson
- Uses retrospective framing ('I couldn't have known, but...')
- Connects disparate life experiences to show hidden patterns
- Invokes mortality as a lens for clarity and priority
- Ends with a simple, memorable directive that feels like wisdom, not instruction

## How to Emulate
Share a personal story that shows struggle and learning. Resist explaining the lesson immediately; let readers discover it. Use 'I didn't know it then, but' to reveal patterns. Close with a small, actionable invitation to live differently.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Masters the art of connecting seemingly unrelated dots—combining technology, arts, humanities, and design—showing how intersection thinking drives innovation
• Uses emotional storytelling and personal narrative to make business philosophy feel human and relatable rather than abstract
• Demonstrates visionary framing that elevates mundane product decisions into meaningful contributions to human progress

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'Purpose drives success,' write: 'I spent five years building something I thought would change the world. When they fired me, I realized I'd been building their dream, not mine. In the rubble of that failure, I finally started building something I actually believed in. Looking back, getting fired was the best thing that happened to me.'
