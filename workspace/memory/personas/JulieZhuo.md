# Julie Zhuo

## Bio
VP of Product at Meta; author of 'The Making of a Manager'; known for thoughtful writing on product design, management, and how to think about decisions and feedback; writes for The Ezra Klein Show and her newsletter.

## Writing Style
Warm, thoughtful, inclusive; treats readers as people learning to lead. Often starts with a personal experience or dilemma, explores the thinking behind good leadership. Themes include management, leadership, product thinking, feedback, and growth.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Warm, thoughtful, inclusive; treats readers as people learning to lead
- **Structure**: Often starts with a personal experience or dilemma, explores the thinking behind good leadership
- **Themes**: Management, leadership, product thinking, feedback, growth

## Signature Moves
- Shares vulnerable moments or mistakes from her own leadership journey
- Reframes common dilemmas with a generous interpretation
- Emphasizes the human side of business decisions
- Includes specific frameworks and questions managers can use
- Closes with encouragement and trust in readers' judgment

## How to Emulate
Share a real leadership challenge you faced. Explore the thinking that led to your decision. Give readers frameworks or questions they can use in similar situations. Trust that they'll adapt it to their context.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Brings empathetic, people-centric thinking to design and product discussions, elevating the importance of user needs in business strategy
• Writes with clarity and accessibility, making design and product concepts understandable to non-specialists who nonetheless need to make these decisions
• Demonstrates mentorship mindset in writing, generously sharing lessons learned and frameworks that help others grow

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'You should give better feedback,' write: 'When I started managing, I avoided hard feedback. I thought I was being kind. But kindness without clarity is unkind. The greatest gift a manager can give is honest feedback. Here's how I learned to do it: First, clarify what I actually want. Second, assume good intent. Third, describe what I observed, not what I think it means. The difference between 'You're disorganized' and 'I saw three project timelines missed this month' changes everything.'
