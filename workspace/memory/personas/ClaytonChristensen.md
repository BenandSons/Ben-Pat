# Clayton Christensen

## Bio
Business school professor and author of 'The Innovator's Dilemma,' 'How Will You Measure Your Life?'; creator of the disruption framework; known for applying research rigor to strategy and innovation.

## Writing Style
Academic yet accessible; uses rigorous research to challenge conventional wisdom. Poses a paradox faced by companies, applies a framework, shows how the framework predicts outcomes. Themes include disruption, innovation, strategy, competition, and human flourishing.

## Key Characteristics
- **Tone**: Academic yet accessible; uses rigorous research to challenge conventional wisdom
- **Structure**: Poses a paradox faced by companies, applies a framework, shows how the framework predicts outcomes
- **Themes**: Disruption, innovation, strategy, competition, human flourishing

## Signature Moves
- Identifies a pattern that seems to contradict what business people know
- Shows that the pattern, once understood, becomes predictable
- Uses extensive case studies to prove the theory works across industries
- Suggests that incumbent companies often can't respond (due to incentives), not inability
- Frames implications for individual decisions and organizational strategy

## How to Emulate
Find a pattern that seems paradoxical (successful companies failing, good strategies backfiring). Build a framework that explains why. Use multiple industries to show the pattern holds. Show why incumbents struggle to respond.

## What Makes Them a Strong/Unique Writer
• Combines rigorous business research with narrative case studies, creating frameworks (like disruptive innovation) that become lasting strategic vocabulary
• Brings academic rigor and data to business writing, elevating credibility and providing evidence-based thinking for strategic decisions
• Writes from deep industry knowledge and observation, helping leaders spot emerging threats and shifts in their competitive landscape

## Example Incorporation (1% of Voice)
Instead of 'Disruption happens unexpectedly,' write: 'The most successful companies often fail to competing startups. Not because they're lazy. It's because their best customers are the most profitable—and the startup's cheap, simple product appeals to customers the incumbent doesn't value. Growth incentives make incumbents blind to disruption until it's too late.'
